Number conventions

When using numbers in a publication, there are various decisions to be made about their presentation, like whether to show them in words or numerals.

Contents

The rule

Several different conventions could be invoked when deciding whether to present numbers in words or numerals. Most of these conventions specify a cut-off point (relating to the size of the number) after which numerals are used, but this point may be set at zero, nine, ten, twenty, one hundred or some other number.

Opening a sentence

When opening a sentence, a number should always be expressed in words.

Example

Fifty-seven people attended the rally.

Sixty per cent of hotel employees are casuals.

If the number is lengthy when expressed in words, it may be better to rearrange the sentence.

Example

Do

The board received 621 complaints.

Don't

Six hundred and twenty-one complaints were received.

Similarly, recasting the sentence is often best if other numbers in the same passage are expressed in numerals; this makes comparisons easier as well as maintaining consistency.

Example

Do

Of the 621 complaints received, 531 related to under-award pay and 90 related to working hours.

Don't

Six hundred and twenty-one complaints were received; of these 531 related to under-award pay and 90 related to working hours.

Where a number that would open a sentence is accompanied by a symbol (for example, a dollar sign) and therefore cannot be expressed in words, the sentence should also be rearranged.

Example

Do

In one suburb alone, a total of $12,684 was collected.

Don't

$12,684 was the total collected from one suburb alone.

You can also find the rules on the punctuation and spacing of numbers (both in words and numerals) in the punctuation section of this manual.

Measurements

Always use numerals for numbers that are accompanied by a symbol.

Example

8°C

3km

45.9s

Even when the measurement unit is written in full, numerals remain the most common choice for the number.

Example

8 degrees Celsius

3 kilometres

45.9 seconds

However, in general discursive text where precise numbers or measurements play little part, words may be appropriate for both the number and measurement.

Example

He was twenty minutes late.

The child was ten or eleven years old.

Mathmatical relationships

Numerals should always be used to express precise mathematical relationships, such as in equations and ratios.

Example

96 + 37 = 133 or 96 plus 37 equals 133

5:1

In general text, words are an option only where the mathematical relationship is not intended to be exact.

Example

Many households—about one in six—are struggling to afford basic necessities.

Large numbers

Aiding recognition

Round numbers up to ten million are readily recognisable, whether words or numerals are used.

Example

two hundred five thousand nine million

200 5000 9,000,000

Larger or more complex numbers expressed solely in either words or numerals can be cumbersome or confusing, so it is often better to use a combination of words and numerals.

Example

Do

2.5 million

Don't

2,500,000

two million, five hundred thousand

An alternative method preferred in much scientific and technical documentation is to use powers of ten.

Example

2.5 x 106 [= 2 500 000]

The terms ‘billion, ‘trillion’ and ‘quadrillion’

The terms billion, trillion and quadrillion were originally used (as their prefixes suggest) to signify a million multiplied by a factor of two, three and four respectively.

Example

billion = million x million (1012)

trillion = million x million x million (1018)

quadrillion = million x million x million x million (1024)
