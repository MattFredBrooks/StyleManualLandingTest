The rule

The particulars of your body copy will be determined by the topic of your page. Regardless of topic, it’s a good practice to follow the inverted pyramid structure when writing copy: Begin with the information that’s most important to your users and then present information of less importance.

Keep each section and subsection focused — a good approach is to include one theme (topic) per section.

Example

1 to 9

one or two of them

10 to 9999

Why it’s easier to read numbers on screen

Norman Nielson Group tells us the science behind why it’s easier to read numbers on screen.

Large numbers

Abbreviate thousand, million, billion and trillion in headings, tables and graphics.

Example

1k

2m

3bn

Note:

If a Rule content type has more than one subrule, then a horizontal light grey line will separate each subrule. The first rule (directly under the H2 rule heading) will not have a horizontal line.

When opening a sentence, a number should always be expressed in words.

Example

Fifty-seven people attended the rally.

Sixty per cent of hotel employees are casuals.

Accessible captions

Add ‘Caption: description of the image…’ below the image.

Example

Do

Do use the figure tag when adding images.

Do add an alt attribute to the img tag that describes the content of the image.

Don't

Don’t duplicate the text in your caption in the alt attribute.

Don’t use the p tag for captions, although this has been an acceptable usage in the past, modern screen readers are optimised for HTML5 and will benefit from proper semantic mark up.

Note:

An example of an Example content type using Design System components Page alerts for the Do and Don't sections.

Screen readers

Screen readers are an assistive technology that helps users who are blind, visually impaired or have reading difficulties.

People use screen readers to navigate and understand web pages through a combination of:

  • reading the content of the web page in a linear order
  • moving around the page through headings, links and form elements

Compliance

All agencies must comply with the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines 2.0 (WCAG 2.0) outlines ways to make digital content accessible to the broadest population.

The Australian Government Digital Service Standard requires conformance to WCAG 2.0 level AA, which includes level A. There are 38 criteria that apply at level AA, plus some from level AAA we recommend.

Content authors are primarily responsible for 17 criteria.
